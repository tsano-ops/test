**2.2.1. Introduction**  
**Record Type:** Family Member Record

**Location:** Me, My Family and My Network → My Family → All Family Categories (Parents, Children, Partner, Former Partner, Siblings, Grandparents, Relatives, etc.)

**Purpose of the Family Member Record**

The Family Member Record is a dedicated, secure, and beautifully organised space for everything related to the people who shape the PlanOwner’s life.  
It brings together identity details, relationships, documents, memories, photos, and personal history — creating a complete and living portrait of each family member.  
Its purpose is to ensure:

**✔ Your family information is organised in a way that supports real-life planning**  
The system can understand your family structure clearly, so it can help with inheritance, guardianship, caregiving, legal documents, and long-term organisation.

**✔ Important documents and details are always accessible when they matter most**  
Birth certificates, medical records, legal documents, notes and key information are stored neatly and can be found instantly—by you today, and by trusted people in the future.

**✔ Continuity and accuracy across every part of your plan**  
All modules that rely on family structure—Family Tree, Will creation, Inheritance Engine, Executor workflows, Post-Loss Support—use this record to stay consistent and correct.

**✔ A single place to preserve memories, stories, and emotional legacy**  
Here you can collect photos, letters, interviews, voice notes, milestones and reflections—creating a meaningful time capsule for your children, partner, parents or anyone important in your life.

**✔ Support for the people who may need to help your family one day**  
In emergencies or after loss, your executors, guardians, or loved ones will know exactly where to find essential information and documents, reducing stress and confusion during difficult moments.

**✔ A unified home for the practical and emotional sides of family life**  
This record connects legal, organisational and sentimental information into one complete portrait of each family member.

**2.2.2. Creation Methods**

A Family Member Record can be created in several different ways, depending on the user flow and context. Each method ensures that the PlanOwner can easily build a complete and accurate picture of their family structure.  The general rule, however, is that a grandparent can be added only after a parent has been added (since the grandparent must be connected through that parent). Users cannot add a grandparent unless the parent exists first. The same goes for the following categories: 

* Grandparent  
* Grandchild  
* Extended Family

Each of these requires a node that connects to the PlanOwner ultimately. Also, when adding a child, the user must point out both parents of the child, even if one of the parents is unknown (in this case a placeholder record title ‘Unknown’ is created).

***2.2.2.1. Automatic Creation During Onboarding or answers in Family & Relationship Card  (System-Generated Records)***

During onboarding, the system may automatically create **placeholder Family Member Records** for close family members identified through the user’s responses.  
Example:

If the user answers:  
 **“How many children do you have?” → 2**  
The system automatically creates:

* **Child 1 – empty record**  
* **Child 2 – empty record**

  The records include:

* a placeholder name (“Child 1”, “Child 2”)  
* an automatically assigned **Relationship Type** (layer 1\)  (Child)  
* no personal data yet

  Purpose of placeholder records

* Motivates the user to complete the missing information later  
* Ensures the Family Tree and inheritance logic remain accurate from day one  
* Creates structure early to support other modules (Medical Info, Emotional Legacy, Will generation)  
* Reduces onboarding friction (the user doesn’t need to fill everything immediately)

  These placeholder records behave exactly like full Family Member Records and can be expanded at any time by opening them.

  Other auto-generated scenarios:

* The user states they live with a partner → **Partner record is created (empty)**  
* The user adds parents during onboarding → **Mother/Father records created**  
* The user chooses to add Family Tree → **system suggests missing nodes** and creates placeholders for them

  This ensures that even partially known family structures are represented correctly and consistently.

The user can intentionally create a new Family Member Record through:

***a. Add Family button***

  ***b. From the Family & Relationships card***

  Inside the PlanOwner Profile or another Family Member Record, the user can create a new related person (e.g., child, spouse, parent) by selecting a relationship type.

  ***c. From the My Family page***

  The “+ Add Family Member” action creates a new record directly from the family overview.


  **d. From the Family Tree**

  ***e. From Link Record flow***

  When linking relationships, the user can choose:

* Link an existing Person Record  
* Create a new Family Member Record

* User adds a guardian in a Will template → *“Create Family Member record for this guardian?”*  
* User mentions a spouse in a Life Event → *“Add this person to My Family?”*

  If the user confirms, a new Family Member Record is created automatically.

**Relationship Engine — System Rules**

**1**. **Relationships are always bidirectional**

When a user creates or links a relationship between **Record A** and **Record B**, the system must create (or update) the **reverse relationship** automatically so both records stay consistent.

* If Record A is connected to Record B, Record B is automatically connected to Record A.  
* The UI may show it “from A’s perspective” or “from B’s perspective”, but it’s the same link. The relationship may be *worded differently* depending on whose record you’re viewing, but it’s still the same connection underneath.

When the user selects a role from the current record’s perspective, the system assigns the correct inverse role on the other record.

Examples:

* From Maria’s record \[anchor record\]: **“Your Biological Son” \[Peter\]**  
* From Peter’s record: **“Peter’s Biological Mother” \[Maria\]**

For relationship types where dates are meaningful (marriage, civil partnership, engagement, adoption, divorce):

* The same key dates are stored on the **relationship link** and therefore appear identically in both records (same precision, no guessing).

* Biological Parent/child or Biological Siblings or Biological Grandparents/Grandchildren relationships generally don’t have a “relationship start date”.

---

**2**. **Creating/Editing one relationship can trigger recalculation of other relationships**

After any relationship is created/edited, the system runs a **Relationship Recalculation** step that can auto-create high-confidence links between other records. These autocreated links are categorised in two tiers to prevent wrong assumptions:

* **Tier A (Auto-create, no confirmation):** only relationships that are logically certain from the graph. Example: Current Husband \- Current Wife.

* **Tier B (Auto-suggest, requires confirmation):** relationships that are plausible but not guaranteed. Example: Child \- Parent (as could be biological parent or step-parent).

---

**3\. Children: spouse-aware capture, but never assumed**

When the anchor record adds a **child relationship** and the anchor already has a **spouse/partner relationship**:

* In the **spouse’s record**, the child is also recorded under **Children** (so the spouse’s family view stays complete).  
* The platform then prompts the user to specify the spouse’s relationship to the child, because the spouse may not be the biological parent.

Typical choices:

* Biological Parent  
* Adoptive Parent  
* Step-Parent

Until the user answers, the spouse–child relationship is treated as **“needs clarification”** and must not be used as biological parent data (especially in the Family Tree visualization).

---

**4\. Siblings**

Sibling relationships are created quickly, but the platform avoids assuming what kind of sibling relationship it is until the user confirms.

#### **4.1 Create the sibling link (immediate, two-way)**

When a user adds a sibling from the anchor record (“Add sibling”):

* The sibling is immediately connected in both directions.  
* Both records show each other under **Siblings** right away.  
* At first, the platform treats this as a **generic sibling link** (“Sibling”) unless it’s already known.

#### **4.2 Confirm the sibling type from the sibling’s record (prompted)**

When the user then views the **sibling’s record**, the platform prompts again:

“Is this a biological sibling, half-sibling, or step-sibling?”

This is intentional: sibling labels are easy to misclassify, and the platform does not guess.

After confirmation:

* Biological → “Biological Brother/Sister/Sibling”  
* Half → “Half-Brother/Half-Sister/Half-Sibling”  
* Step → “Step-Brother/Step-Sister/Step-Sibling”

Until confirmed, the relationship remains **“Sibling — needs clarification.”**

---

**5\. Step and in-law relationships are suggested, not assumed**

Step-siblings, in-laws, and other marriage-derived relationships are easy to get wrong if assumed.

The platform may suggest these relationships when the situation strongly points that way, but should require user confirmation before finalizing.

---

**6\. Conflict handling and upgrades**

When new information is added, the system must resolve conflicts deterministically:

* If a link would create an impossible state (e.g., two “current spouses” where monogamy is assumed), block and require user choice.

* If new info makes a relationship more specific, **upgrade** it:

  * Sibling: unknown → Half → Full (as parents are added)

  * Parent label: Biological Parent → Biological Mother/Father (as sex/gender becomes known)

### **Family Tree visualisation (how it behaves)**

The Family Tree is a **PlanOwner-anchored** view of the family graph. Members can be added directly from the tree using **Quick Add**, but only for people who can be linked **immediately and directly** to the PlanOwner’s record (the anchor). That means that when starting from the anchor, Quick Add in the tree only supports adding a **parent**, **child**, **sibling**, or **partner/spouse**—not extended relatives (like cousins, aunts/uncles, grandparents) until they exist through those direct links.

### **A PlanOwner-centred tree view**

* The Family Tree is visually anchored around the **PlanOwner (“Me”)** in the middle of the tree.

* Family members appear as **profile cards** placed in generations:

  * **Children** appear above the PlanOwner.  
  * **Parents** appear below.  
  * **Grandparents** appear further down (when available).  
  * A **partner/spouse** appears alongside the PlanOwner.

### **Relationship cards (the “nodes”)**

Each person in the tree is shown as a linked summary card with:

* Profile photo/avatar  
* Name  
* Specific Role (Layer 2\) (e.g., Daughter, Son, Mother, Father, Husband)  
* A key date line (e.g., date of birth/date of death)  
* Age (shown in brackets) (age at which died if person deceased)

Cards also have a small **“+”** icon, signalling that the user can **extend the tree from that person** (add or link relatives from their node, depending on permissions/rules).

### **Tree navigation controls**

At the bottom of the tree view, there are simple controls to help users move around the canvas:

* **Center** (re-centres the view on the PlanOwner / main focal point)  
* **Zoom** controls (zoom in/out to handle larger trees)

**Part II. My Family RECORD SYSTEM STRUCTURE** 

This section defines **how any Record works in PlanAfter**. All Record types (PlanOwner, Family Member, Pet, Asset, etc.) follow this same structural logic  
---

**1\. Record (Single Source of Truth**)  
A **Record** is the single authoritative data object representing **one real-world entity**  
 (e.g. a person, family member, pet, asset).  
All information about that entity is:

* stored once  
* governed by **clear ownership rules**  
* edited only inside the Record ( Summary Cards may trigger actions, but they never own or store data.)  
* referenced everywhere else via **read-only projections-**  summary cards

No other component owns data. All system logic — legal, relational, emotional, and operational — depends on this principle.  
---

**2\. Input of data**   
**2.1 Tabs (Top-Level Record Navigation)**  
Tabs organize the Record into **major functional areas**.  
Each tab contains one or more **Input Cards** where data is actually entered and managed.

**Standard Tabs for a Personal Records \- My Profile/ *Family Member/ Pet*** 

* Overview  
* Document Entries  
* Album  
* Life Story  
* Memorial (only if deceased)

**Standard Tabs for a My Network Contacts Record**

* Overview  
* Document Entries  
* Memorial (only if deceased)

Tabs are:

* consistent across records  
* permission-aware  
* lifecycle-aware (e.g. Memorial appears only when applicable)

**2.2. Record Input Cards (Domain Sections)**  
**Record Input Cards** live inside tabs and are the **only place where data is created or edited**.  
They are the only components that:

feed all other system components

* owns a specific domain of information  
* writes directly to the Record and validate inputs  
* feeds multiple system components (Header, Summary Cards, engines)

### **Perspective-Based Relationship Logic**

Family relationships in PlanAfter are perspective-aware.

Each relationship is defined relative to a specific reference record, but may appear differently when viewed from another family member’s perspective.

To support accurate family trees, inheritance logic, and user understanding, PlanAfter separates relationships into three conceptual layers:

**Layer 1 – Relationship Type**  
 A system-level classification (e.g. Parent, Child, Sibling).  
 This layer is used internally for logic and is visible only in Edit Mode.

**Layer 2 – Specific Role**  
 A user-facing, human-readable role (e.g. Mother, Daughter, Aunt).  
 This role is always displayed relative to the currently viewed record.

**Layer 3 – Timeline Conditional Dates**  
 Optional dates that describe when a relationship started or ended, shown only when relevant.

When a relationship is created from the perspective of one family member, the system stores the relationship relative to that record.  
 When the same person is viewed from another family member’s record, PlanAfter automatically derives and displays the correct role for that perspective.

Example:  
 If a person is added as “Daughter” from the record of a grandmother, that same person will appear as “Aunt” when viewed from the PlanOwner’s record, with optional contextual clarification (e.g. “daughter of Maria Ivanova”).

Derived roles are computed dynamically and are never stored as source-of-truth data.
